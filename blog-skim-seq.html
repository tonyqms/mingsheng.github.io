Skim-Seq + Imputation: Cost-Effective Genotyping at Scale

üìÖ August 10, 2024 ‚è±Ô∏è 12 min read ‚úçÔ∏è Mingsheng Qi, Ph.D.

A technical breakdown of how low-coverage sequencing paired with reference panels delivers 95% accuracy at 10% of traditional WGS costs.

Whole-genome sequencing (WGS) has become the gold standard for comprehensive genotyping in crops and other organisms. But there's a problem: it's expensive. At 20-30X coverage, sequencing thousands of individuals for breeding or GWAS studies can quickly burn through a budget.

Enter skim sequencing plus imputation‚Äîa strategy that sequences at ultra-low coverage (0.1-1X) and computationally "fills in" the missing data using reference panels. When done right, it delivers 95%+ genotype accuracy at a fraction of the cost. I've deployed this approach across multiple crop species (sweetcorn, popcorn, soybean), and it's become a cornerstone of modern breeding programs.

This post breaks down the technical details: how it works, when it works well (and when it doesn't), and practical tips for implementation.

The Economics: Why This Matters

Let's start with the business case. Traditional WGS at 20X coverage costs roughly $100-200 per sample (depending on genome size and sequencing platform). For a breeding population of 5,000 individuals, that's $500K-1M just for genotyping.

Skim-seq at 0.5X coverage? Maybe $10-20 per sample. Same population: $50K-100K. That's an 80-90% cost reduction.

Cost Comparison (per sample):

  • 30X WGS: $150-300
  • 10X WGS: $50-100
  • 1X skim + imputation: $15-30
  • 0.1X skim + imputation: $5-10

The catch? You need a high-quality reference panel. More on that later.

How It Works: The Technical Foundation

Step 1: Skim Sequencing

You sequence your samples at very low coverage‚Äîtypically 0.1X to 1X. At this depth, you're only observing each genomic position in a tiny fraction of reads. Most sites have zero coverage; some have one read; very few have multiple reads.

This sounds catastrophically bad for variant calling‚Äîand it is, if you try to call variants de novo. But that's not what we're doing.

Step 2: Genotype Likelihoods

Instead of hard-calling genotypes (AA, AB, BB), we calculate genotype likelihoods at known variant positions. Given the observed reads, what's the probability this individual is homozygous reference? Heterozygous? Homozygous alternate?

At 0.5X coverage, these probabilities are often weak. If you see one read with the reference allele, it could be a true homozygote or a heterozygote where you just didn't sample the alternate allele. The likelihood scores reflect this uncertainty.

Step 3: Imputation

This is where the magic happens. Using a reference panel of deeply sequenced individuals from the same population, imputation algorithms (Beagle, Impute2, Minimac) leverage linkage disequilibrium (LD) to infer the most likely genotype at each position.

The logic: if you observe an individual has genotype A at SNP1 and genotype C at SNP3, and the reference panel shows that this haplotype combination always has genotype G at SNP2, you can impute SNP2 with high confidence‚Äîeven if you have zero coverage there.

"Imputation doesn't create information from nothing‚Äîit borrows information from reference individuals via shared haplotype structure."

When Does This Work Well?

Skim-seq + imputation isn't universally applicable. Success depends on several factors:

1. High-Quality Reference Panel

This is the linchpin. Your reference panel should:

  • Be large enough: 100-500+ individuals minimum, depending on population diversity. More is better.
  • Represent your target population: If you're imputing tropical maize, a temperate maize panel won't cut it.
  • Be deeply sequenced: 20-30X coverage to ensure accurate variant calls.
  • Capture rare variants: Common SNPs (MAF > 0.05) impute well with modest panels; rare variants need larger panels.

In our popcorn project, we built a custom reference panel of 300 diverse lines sequenced at 25X. This enabled accurate imputation even at 0.1X skim coverage for downstream individuals.

2. Strong Linkage Disequilibrium

LD is what makes imputation possible. In highly inbred or clonal populations (many crops, lab mice), LD extends over long distances, and imputation is highly accurate.

In outbred populations with extensive recombination (humans, wild populations), LD decays quickly, and you need:

  • Larger reference panels
  • Higher skim-seq coverage
  • Denser marker spacing

3. Known Variant Positions

Imputation works at sites where variants are already cataloged in your reference panel. You can't impute structural variants or novel SNPs that aren't in the panel.

For de novo variant discovery, you still need deep sequencing.

Practical Implementation

Building the Reference Panel

Start here. The panel is your foundation.

  1. Select representative individuals: Diversity matters more than size up to a point. Sample across your population structure.
  2. Deep sequencing: 20-30X coverage. Don't skimp‚Äîerrors in the panel propagate to all imputed samples.
  3. Rigorous QC: Filter variants carefully. False positives in the panel create systematic errors downstream.
  4. Phasing: Use tools like Beagle or ShapeIT to phase your panel into haplotypes. This dramatically improves imputation accuracy.

Skim-Seq Library Prep and Sequencing

Surprisingly straightforward. Standard Illumina library prep, but multiplexed to high levels (96-384 samples per lane) to achieve low cost-per-sample.

Key considerations:

  • PCR-free libraries: Preferred if budget allows. Reduces GC bias and improves coverage uniformity.
  • Read length: Paired-end 150bp is standard. Longer reads help with alignment but aren't essential.
  • Insert size: 300-500bp works well for most genomes.

Alignment and Genotype Likelihoods

Align reads to the reference genome with BWA-MEM or minimap2. Mark duplicates (but don't remove them at ultra-low coverage‚Äîevery read counts).

Calculate genotype likelihoods at known variant sites using:

  • GATK HaplotypeCaller: Slow but thorough
  • bcftools mpileup: Fast, good for large cohorts
  • ANGSD: Explicitly designed for low-coverage data

Export likelihoods in VCF or Beagle format for imputation.

Imputation

I primarily use Beagle 5.x for crop genomes. It's fast, accurate, and handles large datasets well.

Basic workflow:

  1. Phase your reference panel with Beagle or Eagle
  2. Run imputation with your skim-seq samples, providing genotype likelihoods (not hard calls)
  3. Beagle outputs posterior probabilities for each genotype‚Äîuse these for downstream QC

For human genomes, tools like Minimac4 (via Michigan Imputation Server) are popular. For non-model organisms, Beagle's flexibility is hard to beat.

Quality Control

Post-imputation QC is critical. Key metrics:

QC Metrics to Monitor:

  • Imputation quality score (R¬≤): Beagle reports this per-site. Filter sites with R¬≤ < 0.3-0.5 depending on application.
  • Dosage R¬≤ vs. called R¬≤: Compare imputed dosages to a subset of high-coverage validation samples.
  • MAF distribution: Rare variants (MAF < 0.01) impute poorly at low coverage. Filter aggressively if needed.
  • Sample-level concordance: Sequence a few samples at both skim and deep coverage to benchmark accuracy.

Real-World Performance

In our sweetcorn QTL mapping project, we skim-sequenced 800 F2 individuals at 0.5X coverage using a 200-sample reference panel (30X).

Results:

  • Imputation accuracy (R¬≤): 0.91 for common SNPs (MAF > 0.05), 0.76 for rare SNPs (MAF 0.01-0.05)
  • Cost savings: 85% reduction vs. 10X WGS
  • QTL detection: Identical LOD peaks compared to deep-sequenced validation cohort

For popcorn breeding, we went even lower: 0.1X coverage, 300-sample reference panel, 2,000 breeding lines genotyped.

Results:

  • Accuracy: R¬≤ = 0.87 for common SNPs
  • Cost: $8/sample
  • Impact: Enabled genomic selection at scale‚Äîpreviously cost-prohibitive

Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

Pitfall 1: Inadequate Reference Panel

Trying to impute with a small or unrepresentative panel is the #1 reason for failure. If your panel has 50 samples and doesn't capture your population's diversity, you'll get poor results no matter how sophisticated your imputation algorithm.

Solution: Invest in the reference panel first. It's reusable across projects and pays for itself quickly.

Pitfall 2: Using Hard Genotype Calls

At low coverage, hard-calling genotypes (e.g., with GATK's standard pipeline) discards critical information. A site with one read might be called homozygous with high confidence, but it's actually highly uncertain.

Solution: Always use genotype likelihoods or posterior probabilities as input to imputation.

Pitfall 3: Over-Interpreting Rare Variants

Rare variants impute poorly, especially at ultra-low coverage. Don't trust a singleton (MAF = 0.0005) in your skim-seq cohort.

Solution: Filter by MAF and imputation quality. For rare variant studies, you need higher skim coverage or targeted sequencing.

Pitfall 4: Ignoring Population Structure

If your skim-seq samples span multiple subpopulations and your reference panel only represents one, imputation accuracy will be subpopulation-dependent.

Solution: Either build a cosmopolitan reference panel or stratify imputation by population.

When NOT to Use Skim-Seq + Imputation

This approach isn't a panacea. Situations where it fails or is suboptimal:

  • De novo variant discovery: You need deep sequencing to find novel variants.
  • Structural variant detection: Imputation is primarily for SNPs and small indels.
  • No suitable reference panel: Non-model organisms without existing genomic resources.
  • Extremely diverse or admixed populations: LD is too weak for accurate imputation.
  • Need for rare variant association: Unless you're willing to go to 2-5X skim coverage.

The Future: Where Is This Heading?

Skim-seq + imputation is mature technology, but improvements continue:

  • Better algorithms: Machine learning approaches (e.g., deep learning imputation) show promise for low-LD populations.
  • Pangenome references: Using graph-based references instead of linear genomes improves alignment and imputation in diverse populations.
  • Hybrid approaches: Combining skim WGS with targeted sequencing for specific regions of interest.
  • Long-read skim-seq: PacBio and Nanopore at low coverage for structural variant-aware imputation.

Practical Recommendations

If you're considering skim-seq + imputation:

  1. Start with a pilot: Sequence 50-100 samples at both skim and deep coverage. Validate imputation accuracy before scaling up.
  2. Invest in the reference panel: This is your rate-limiting step. A great panel unlocks years of cost savings.
  3. Benchmark against your use case: GWAS is forgiving of imputation errors; fine-mapping is not. Know your tolerance.
  4. Budget for QC: Post-imputation filtering and validation are essential‚Äîdon't treat imputed genotypes as ground truth without checking.
  5. Document everything: Future you will thank present you for clear records of panel composition, software versions, and filtering criteria.

Conclusion

Skim-seq + imputation has democratized large-scale genomics in crops and other species. What was once a million-dollar genotyping project can now be done for $50K-100K, enabling breeding programs, GWAS, and population genetics at unprecedented scale.

But it's not magic‚Äîit's a tradeoff. You gain cost-efficiency and throughput at the expense of rare variant detection and de novo discovery. Know when to use it, invest in a quality reference panel, and validate rigorously.

For breeding and association mapping, it's hard to beat. For fine-mapping and functional studies, you'll still need deep sequencing. The key is matching the tool to the question.

"In genomics, as in life, you rarely get something for nothing. But with skim-seq + imputation, you get pretty close."
