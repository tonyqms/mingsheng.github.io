Building Scientific Services That Scale

üìÖ November 5, 2024 ‚è±Ô∏è 9 min read ‚úçÔ∏è Mingsheng Qi, Ph.D.

What makes a genomics service platform successful? Balancing custom solutions with standardized offerings, and why SLAs matter more than you think.

Running a scientific services platform‚Äîwhether it's sequencing, genotyping, or bioinformatics‚Äîis fundamentally different from running an internal lab. You're not just generating data for your own projects; you're productizing science for external clients who have their own timelines, budgets, and success criteria.

I've been on both sides: as a core facility user frustrated by black-box processes and unclear timelines, and as a service provider managing 40+ concurrent projects with diverse client needs. The patterns that separate successful platforms from struggling ones aren't always obvious‚Äîbut they're remarkably consistent.

The Fundamental Tension: Customization vs. Standardization

Every scientific service lives in the tension between these two forces:

  • Clients want customization: "Can you use this specific aligner?" "We need to add one more QC step." "Our samples are unique and need special handling."
  • Operations demand standardization: Reproducible workflows, predictable turnaround times, scalable processes.

Get this balance wrong, and you'll either become a chaotic job shop that can't scale, or a rigid factory that loses clients to competitors who "actually listen."

"The best service platforms offer 80% standardization with clearly defined 20% customization points."

Service Architecture: Tiered Offerings

The solution? Tiered service structures that separate commoditized offerings from bespoke work.

Tier 1: Standard Packages

These are your bread-and-butter services: fixed scope, fixed price, fixed timeline. Examples:

  • Whole genome sequencing (30X coverage, standard library prep, 3-week turnaround)
  • RNA-seq (stranded, 30M reads/sample, standard DESeq2 analysis)
  • 16S microbiome profiling (V4 region, QIIME2 pipeline)

Client benefit: Predictable cost and timeline. Clear deliverables.
Your benefit: High-throughput, optimized workflows. Minimal handholding.

Tier 2: Custom Analysis

Standard data generation, custom analysis. Client gets data from Tier 1 service, but needs specialized downstream work: custom GWAS models, multi-omics integration, novel statistical approaches.

Pricing: Time & materials, with scope-of-work agreement.
Timeline: Iterative, with milestones.

Tier 3: Full Bespoke

Everything is negotiable: experimental design, assay development, novel workflows. This is consulting + execution.

Warning: These projects are high-touch, unpredictable, and can sink your operations if they dominate your pipeline. Price them accordingly (2-3x standard rates) and cap the percentage of your capacity devoted to them (20-30% max).

The Critical Role of SLAs (Service Level Agreements)

Here's what I learned the hard way: vague promises kill client trust faster than slow turnarounds.

Early in my current role, we'd tell clients "Your results will be ready in 2-3 weeks." Then we'd hit an instrument issue, and it would stretch to 4 weeks. The client wasn't upset about the delay‚Äîthey were upset that we didn't communicate it proactively, and they had no way to plan around it.

What Makes a Good SLA?

Components of Effective SLAs:

  1. Defined trigger: When does the clock start? Upon sample receipt? After QC pass?
  2. Clear deliverable: What exactly do they get? Raw data? Analyzed results? A report?
  3. Measurable timeline: "10 business days" not "about 2 weeks"
  4. QC gates: What happens if samples fail QC? Who pays for re-runs?
  5. Communication cadence: How often do you update clients? Weekly? At milestones?
  6. Escalation path: What if you miss the SLA? Refund? Rush processing on next project?

We now publish SLAs for all Tier 1 services. Our on-time delivery rate improved from 65% to 92%‚Äînot because our processes got faster, but because we stopped overpromising and started managing expectations.

Operational Excellence: The Unsexy Stuff That Matters

1. Sample Tracking and Chain of Custody

You'd think this is basic, but I've seen too many facilities struggle with "Where is my sample?" inquiries. Invest in:

  • LIMS or LIMS-like tracking: Benchling, LabArchives, or even well-structured databases. Every sample touchpoint gets logged.
  • Barcoding: 2D barcodes for tubes, plates, and racks. Scanners at every workstation.
  • Client-facing dashboards: Let clients check status themselves rather than emailing you constantly.

2. Process Documentation

Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) aren't just for regulatory compliance‚Äîthey're how you scale and train new staff.

Every Tier 1 service should have:

  • Step-by-step wet-lab protocol
  • Computational pipeline documentation (inputs, outputs, software versions)
  • QC criteria and decision trees
  • Troubleshooting guide

If your process only exists in one person's head, you don't have a service‚Äîyou have a single point of failure.

3. Quality Control Frameworks

QC is where good services separate from great ones. You need:

  • Input QC: Reject bad samples early. Angry client up front > catastrophic failure later.
  • Process QC: Positive/negative controls at every step. Monitor instrument performance.
  • Output QC: Automated checks before data delivery. Never let a client discover a failed sample.

We auto-generate QC reports for every project‚Äîcoverage distributions, mapping rates, contamination checks. Clients get these with their data delivery, which builds trust even when there are minor issues.

Pricing: The Hardest Part

Pricing scientific services is part art, part science, part psychology.

Cost-Plus vs. Value-Based

Cost-plus: Calculate your costs (reagents, labor, overhead) and add margin. Simple, but leaves money on the table for high-value services.

Value-based: Price based on client value. A 2-week turnaround might be worth 50% more to a client racing a competitor. But harder to justify and can feel arbitrary.

My approach: Cost-plus for Tier 1, value-based for Tier 3. Be transparent about why custom work costs more (setup time, risk, opportunity cost of tying up resources).

The Minimum Viable Project Size

Small projects kill margins. Setup costs are fixed whether you're running 10 samples or 100. Below a certain threshold, you're losing money.

Solutions:

  • Minimum order size: "We require minimum 24 samples for this service."
  • Small-project surcharge: "$500 setup fee for projects under 50 samples."
  • Batching: "We run this assay quarterly. Next run is October 15."

Counterintuitive, but raising minimums often increases satisfaction‚Äîclients who clear the bar are serious and invested.

Client Communication: The Multiplier

Technical excellence is table stakes. What differentiates top-tier services?

Proactive Updates

Don't make clients ask "Where are my results?" Send automatic updates:

  • Sample received and passed QC
  • Sequencing in progress
  • Analysis complete, QC passed, data available
  • DELAY ALERT: Instrument issue, new ETA is [date]

We built a simple email notification system tied to our pipeline statuses. Client satisfaction scores jumped 20% without changing any technical processes.

Clear Data Deliverables

Nothing frustrates clients more than "Here's a link to your data"‚Äîand it's 50 unlabeled files in a directory structure they don't understand.

Good deliverable packages include:

  • README explaining every file
  • Summary report (PDF) with QC metrics, sample stats, and interpretation notes
  • Raw data (FASTQ, BAM, etc.) with clear naming conventions
  • Processed results (VCF, count matrices, etc.)
  • Analysis code/pipeline used (reproducibility!)

Scaling Challenges

When to Say No

The hardest lesson: not every client is worth having.

Red flags:

  • Client wants a 50% discount because "this is just a pilot"
  • Scope creep in scoping phase ("Oh, we also need...")
  • Requests for extensive hand-holding or training (you're not a consultant, you're a service)
  • Unrealistic timelines ("We need this in 3 days for a grant deadline")

Politely decline or re-route to Tier 3 (with appropriate pricing). Protecting your operational bandwidth is strategic.

Capacity Planning

Successful services operate at 70-85% capacity. Below 70%, you're inefficient. Above 85%, you're one hiccup from missing SLAs.

Build buffers:

  • Reserve 10-15% capacity for troubleshooting and re-runs
  • Block time for instrument maintenance
  • Plan staff vacations around project timelines

The Metrics That Matter

KPIs for Service Platforms:

  • On-time delivery rate: % of projects meeting SLA (target: >90%)
  • Sample pass rate: % of samples clearing QC (monitor for systematic issues)
  • Client NPS (Net Promoter Score): Would they recommend you?
  • Repeat business rate: % of clients returning within 12 months
  • Revenue per FTE: Efficiency metric‚Äîare you scaling or just adding headcount?
  • Margin by service tier: Which offerings are profitable? Which aren't?

Building for the Future

The genomics services market is maturing. What used to be specialty offerings (RNA-seq, ChIP-seq) are now commodities. Differentiation increasingly comes from:

  • Speed: Can you deliver in days, not weeks?
  • Integration: Can you combine multiple data types seamlessly?
  • Insight: Are you delivering data or answers?
  • Reliability: Do you hit your SLAs consistently?

The platforms winning are those that think like product companies, not academic cores. They obsess over client experience, operational metrics, and continuous improvement.

Final Thoughts

Building a successful scientific services platform requires balancing competing demands: customization vs. standardization, throughput vs. quality, growth vs. operational stability.

But the core principle is simple: make it easy for clients to work with you, and hard for them to leave.

Do that, and the technical excellence will compound into sustained business success.
