The Art of Cross-Functional Communication

üìÖ February 18, 2025 ‚è±Ô∏è 11 min read ‚úçÔ∏è Mingsheng Qi, Ph.D.

How to translate between wet-lab biologists, software engineers, and business stakeholders‚Äîwithout losing your mind or the message.

You're in a project meeting. The molecular biologist says, "We need better genotype data." The software engineer asks, "What's the acceptance criteria?" The project manager wants to know, "When will it be done?" And you‚Äîthe bioinformatician‚Äîrealize you're the only person in the room who speaks all three languages.

This is the hidden superpower of successful bioinformatics professionals: not algorithmic brilliance or coding wizardry, but the ability to translate between technical domains and human contexts. After 14 years working at the intersection of biology, computation, and business, I've learned that communication skills often matter more than technical skills.

Here's what I wish someone had taught me on day one.

The Three Languages of Biotech

Every organization operates in at least three distinct communication modes, each with its own vocabulary, priorities, and mental models.

Language 1: The Biologist

Mental model: The world is organisms, phenotypes, and mechanisms. Questions are biological: "Why does this mutant show reduced yield?" "Which genes are upregulated under drought stress?"

Priorities: Biological signal, experimental design, mechanistic understanding. Suspicious of "black box" computational methods. Values interpretability over performance.

Communication style: Narrative, hypothesis-driven. Wants to know "what this means" for their biological system.

Language 2: The Engineer

Mental model: The world is systems, pipelines, and specifications. Questions are technical: "What's the schema?" "How does this scale?" "What are the failure modes?"

Priorities: Reproducibility, modularity, testing. Suspicious of "it works on my laptop." Values robustness over novelty.

Communication style: Precise, specification-driven. Wants clear inputs, outputs, and edge cases defined.

Language 3: The Business Stakeholder

Mental model: The world is outcomes, timelines, and resources. Questions are strategic: "What's the ROI?" "When can we go to market?" "What's the risk?"

Priorities: Business impact, predictability, cost. Suspicious of "we need more time to optimize." Values delivery over perfection.

Communication style: Results-oriented, metric-focused. Wants to know "what changes" and "by when."

The Translation Problem

The challenge isn't that these groups speak different languages‚Äîit's that they're often unaware they're speaking different languages. A biologist saying "we need better data" might mean:

  • Higher sequencing depth (technical)
  • Better experimental controls (experimental design)
  • Different statistical model (analytical)
  • More biological replicates (resource allocation)

Your job as the translator is to disambiguate‚Äîto probe until you understand what they actually need, then translate that need into specifications for engineers, timelines for managers, and analysis plans for yourself.

"The best bioinformaticians don't just analyze data‚Äîthey decode what people are really asking for."

Communication Patterns That Work

Pattern 1: Start with the Question, Not the Method

Bad: "I ran DESeq2 with a Wald test and adjusted for batch effects using ComBat."

Good: "You wanted to know which genes change expression under drought. We found 347 genes significantly upregulated. The top candidate is a known stress-response transcription factor, which validates our approach."

Why it works: You've anchored the conversation to the biological question, demonstrated the result, then offered validation. The method is supporting evidence, not the headline.

Pattern 2: Translate Technical Constraints into Business Terms

When talking to stakeholders, don't explain why the pipeline is slow‚Äîexplain the tradeoff.

Bad: "Alignment is the bottleneck. We're I/O bound on this dataset."

Good: "We can deliver the analysis in 2 weeks at standard depth, or 4 weeks if you need rare variant detection. The rare variant analysis catches 30% more events but requires deeper sequencing. Which timeline works better for your decision point?"

Why it works: You've framed it as a business decision, not a technical constraint. You've given them agency.

Pattern 3: Use Visuals to Bridge Gaps

A picture is worth exponentially more than a thousand words when talking across domains.

Effective Visuals for Different Audiences:

  • For biologists: Heatmaps, volcano plots, pathway diagrams‚Äîthings that map to biological intuition
  • For engineers: System diagrams, flowcharts, DAGs (directed acyclic graphs) of pipeline dependencies
  • For stakeholders: Simple bar charts, timelines, before/after comparisons‚Äîfocus on the delta, not the details

I've salvaged more meetings with a quick Plotly visualization than with any amount of verbal explanation.

Handling Difficult Conversations

When a Biologist Says: "Can't you just fix it in the analysis?"

This usually means: "I have bad data and I'm hoping computation can save me."

Trap response: "No, the data is too noisy. GIGO." (Antagonistic, unhelpful)

Better response: "Let's look at the QC together. I see the issue‚Äîbatch effects are stronger than treatment effects. We have two options: (1) re-run with better balance, which would take X weeks, or (2) proceed with statistical correction, but we'll lose power and might miss real effects. What's more important: timeline or sensitivity?"

Why it works: You've diagnosed the problem, offered options with tradeoffs, and given them ownership of the decision. You're collaborating, not gatekeeping.

When an Engineer Says: "This pipeline needs to be refactored"

This usually means: "This code is messy and I don't want to maintain it."

Trap response: "It works fine, don't break it." (Defensive, short-sighted)

Better response: "I agree the code is fragile‚Äîit grew organically. What's the biggest pain point? If we invest a sprint to modularize X, would that unblock you for feature Y? Let's prioritize: what breaks most often?"

Why it works: You've acknowledged the concern, agreed to the problem, and negotiated scope. You're problem-solving together.

When a Stakeholder Says: "Why is this taking so long?"

This usually means: "I don't understand what's involved and I'm worried about the timeline."

Trap response: "Bioinformatics is complicated." (Condescending, unhelpful)

Better response: "Good question‚Äîlet me break down the timeline. [Show visual] We're here [point]. The sequencing finished on time, but QC flagged 10% of samples as low quality. We're re-running those, which adds a week. The analysis itself is 3 days once we have clean data. So new ETA is [date]. Does that work for your deadline?"

Why it works: You've made the invisible visible, explained the delay cause (external, not your fault), and given them a new concrete date to plan around.

The Meeting Patterns You'll Encounter

The Kickoff Meeting

Your goal: Align on biological question, technical approach, and success criteria.

Key questions to ask:

  • "What decision will you make with these results?"
  • "What would a 'success' result look like?"
  • "What's your timeline and is there flexibility?"
  • "Who else needs to weigh in before we start?"

The Status Update

Your goal: Report progress without overwhelming people with details.

Effective structure:

  1. Status: On track / delayed / ahead
  2. Key progress: One sentence on what was accomplished
  3. Blockers: What you need from others, if anything
  4. Next steps: What you'll work on next

Total time: 2 minutes. If they want details, they'll ask.

The Results Presentation

Your goal: Answer the original question, show the data, and guide interpretation.

Effective structure:

  1. Reminder of the question: "You asked whether gene X affects trait Y."
  2. The answer: "Yes, and the effect is strong."
  3. The evidence: [Show data]
  4. Caveats: "This is in controlled conditions. Field trials will be needed to confirm."
  5. Next steps: "I recommend we sequence 200 more lines to fine-map the region."

Writing for Cross-Functional Audiences

Most of your communication won't be in meetings‚Äîit'll be in Slack messages, emails, and reports. Here's how to write effectively:

The Email Template

Subject: [Project name] - [Status/Question/Results]

TL;DR: One sentence summary of the key point

Context: Brief reminder of what this is about

Update/Question/Findings: The meat of your message

Action needed: What you need from them, with deadline

Technical details: [Optional section, collapsed or at bottom]

Busy stakeholders will read the TL;DR and action items. Technical collaborators will read the whole thing.

The Analysis Report

Structure matters. Here's a template that works:

  1. Executive Summary: 1 paragraph. Question, answer, confidence level.
  2. Key Findings: 3-5 bullet points with supporting visuals.
  3. Methodology: Brief, for reproducibility. Link to detailed methods.
  4. Detailed Results: For those who want to dig deeper.
  5. Limitations and Caveats: Be honest about what you can't conclude.
  6. Recommendations: What should happen next?

Common Communication Failures (and How to Avoid Them)

Failure 1: The Information Dump

Symptom: You send a 10-page report with 47 figures. No one reads it.

Fix: Start with a 1-page summary. Offer to walk through details in a meeting. Most people want answers, not methods.

Failure 2: The Technical Defensiveness

Symptom: Someone questions your approach, and you respond with a lecture on statistical theory.

Fix: Acknowledge their concern, explain your reasoning in their terms, and invite alternatives. "That's a fair question. I used method X because of Y. If you're concerned about Z, we could also try..."

Failure 3: The Scope Creep Enabler

Symptom: Stakeholder asks for "just one more analysis." You say yes. Project never ends.

Fix: "I can do that‚Äîit would take about a week and delay the final report to [date]. Is that okay, or should we consider this a follow-up project?"

Failure 4: The Silent Struggle

Symptom: You hit a blocker, spend days troubleshooting alone, miss deadline.

Fix: Surface problems early. "Quick update: ran into an issue with X. Investigating, but if not resolved by tomorrow, timeline shifts by Y. Wanted to give you heads up."

Building Your Translation Skills

Cross-functional communication isn't innate‚Äîit's a skill you develop deliberately.

Practice Active Listening

When someone makes a request, pause before responding. Ask clarifying questions:

  • "Can you give me an example of what you're imagining?"
  • "What would you do with this information if you had it?"
  • "Is there a specific threshold or outcome that would change your decision?"

Learn the Other Disciplines

Spend time with:

  • Biologists in the lab‚Äîwatch how they think about experiments
  • Engineers in code reviews‚Äîunderstand their quality standards
  • PMs in planning meetings‚Äîsee how they prioritize and manage risk

The goal isn't to become expert in those domains‚Äîit's to develop empathy for their constraints and priorities.

Get Feedback on Your Communication

After a presentation or report, ask: "Was that clear? What could I have explained better?"

You'll be surprised what people found confusing.

When Communication Breaks Down

Sometimes, despite your best efforts, you can't bridge the gap. Recognize when to escalate:

  • Fundamental disagreement on priorities (get leadership involved)
  • Technical misunderstanding that won't resolve (bring in a neutral expert)
  • Scope disputes (document expectations, get sign-off)

It's not failure to ask for help‚Äîit's pragmatism.

The Payoff

Strong cross-functional communication skills compound over time. You become:

  • The trusted advisor people come to for guidance, not just analysis
  • The project unlocker who keeps things moving when others are stuck
  • The culture builder who models how different teams should collaborate

And practically? You get more interesting projects, faster career advancement, and far less frustration from miscommunication.

"Technical skills get you hired. Communication skills get you promoted."

Final Thoughts

Bioinformatics is, at its core, a translation profession. We translate between molecules and models, between data and decisions, between what computers can do and what biologists need.

Master this translation, and you don't just deliver analyses‚Äîyou deliver understanding. And that's what makes you indispensable.
